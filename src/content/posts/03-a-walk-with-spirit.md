title: "A Walk With Spirit"
date: 2026-03-31
description: "The healing in unproductive moments"

---

Most beliefs aren’t formed by direct experience. They are picked up from other people.

I remember playing football one afternoon when something strange happened. One person suddenly started running off the pitch. The rest of us followed almost immediately. No one asked why. No one turned back to check what was behind us. That was just how it worked. You react first, then figure it out later.

Later, we found out there were dogs nearby. They weren’t chasing anyone. They weren’t even paying attention to us.

But by then, it didn’t matter. The reaction had already done its job.

That’s how I learned to be afraid of dogs. Not from being bitten, but by watching how other people behaved.

At first, the belief didn’t feel like mine. It was just something I heard and saw repeatedly. Dogs are dangerous. Dogs will chase you. Dogs will bite you. After a while, it stopped feeling external. I didn’t question it. I just carried it.

Years later, I came across a [Golden Retriever](https://www.akc.org/dog-breeds/golden-retriever/) video while scrolling on Instagram and loved it. My perspective on dogs started to change. I started noticing how lovely dogs are and how not every dog matched the version I had in my head.

Last year, I randomly signed up for a dog-walking volunteer program at a fair. It felt casual at the time. Just something to try. Something different.

Months passed. I forgot about it. Then an email came in. I attended the onboarding. I picked my availability. Small actions, one after the other, without really pausing to think about what they added up to.

A few days later, I was assigned to a dog named Spirit. A ten year old female [Northern Inuit](https://www.nisociety.net/about-the-breed-1). I remember sitting there for a while, looking up the breed. Their size. Their temperament. Trying to prepare for something I still hadn’t fully processed.

Then came the first walk. 

When the door opened, for a second, the fear came rushing back. But then I saw her.

She was large, almost wolf-like just like I had read, but calm. She didn’t bark or rush toward anyone. She moved slowly between the volunteers, one by one, until she reached me.

I expected to feel tense. Instead, I felt calm.

As she leaned in, the fear didn’t just leave. It was replaced by a quiet sense of safety. I just stood there and let the moment happen.

A few minutes later, someone asked if I wanted to take the lead. I said yes before I could think much about it. 

Then I realized I had no idea where we’re headed. She did.

She walked with a steady, deliberate pace, like she’d done this many times before. I followed her. Not just physically, but mentally too. I wasn’t trying to stay in control or anticipate what could go wrong.

I was just walking.

After a while, I noticed how quiet my mind had become. No background stress. No urge to check my phone. Nothing pulling my attention elsewhere. Just the rhythm of walking, the sound of her steps and the occasional glance back to make sure I was still there.

For that hour, everything narrowed to the length of a leash.

There’s a kind of clarity that comes from doing something with no real goal. I used to plan my days tightly, trying to make every hour count. But walking with Spirit didn’t feel like something to optimize. It was just something to be in. 

It also made something else clear. The belief I’d carried for years had never been tested. It had just been repeated.

It only took one direct experience to start undoing it.

That makes me wonder how many other things are like that. How many fears or assumptions feel real, but only exist because we’ve practiced believing them.

That day, I signed up to walk a dog. 

But it felt more like the dog was the one taking my mind for a walk, leading me somewhere I hadn’t been in a long time.
